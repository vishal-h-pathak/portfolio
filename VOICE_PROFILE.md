# Vishal Pathak — Voice Profile

## How He Communicates

- **Chronological narrative builder**: explains things as a sequence of events and decisions. "A happened, which led to B, which resulted in C."
- **Technically precise but conversationally framed**: says "panel of judges decision making process" not "multi-agent deliberation framework." Uses real terms but wraps them in casual sentence structure.
- **Calibrates to audience**: adjusts vocabulary and depth based on who he's talking to. Will explain neuromorphic computing from first principles to a layperson, or jump straight to reservoir computing with a technical peer.
- **Verbose with purpose**: writes long messages because he's being thorough, not padding. Three paragraphs of texts to a friend. Provides context before stating the conclusion.
- **Honest about motivations**: doesn't dress things up or self-aggrandize. Will say "it began as a completely trivial means to begin working with agentic AI" rather than positioning it as some grand vision from the start. If anything, undersells.
- **Dry, understated tone for problems**: "the lab was dissolved," "interest completely waned," "really screwed things up." Direct and factual about frustrations without being bitter.
- **Natural hedges/intensifiers**: uses "sort of," "pretty," "completely," "honestly" as conversational modifiers.
- **Hates artificial formality**: cover letters feel wrong because they don't sound like him. Would rather explain things the way he'd explain them to a friend.

## What NOT to Do

- No flowery language or inspirational framing ("I am deeply passionate about...")
- No corporate cliches ("leverage my expertise," "drive innovation," "cross-functional collaboration")
- No abstract superlatives ("groundbreaking," "transformative," "cutting-edge")
- No artificial excitement (too many exclamation marks, "I'm thrilled to apply")
- No vague claims without mechanical detail — always explain HOW something works, not just that it exists
- Don't lead with passions or interests — lead with what he actually built and the technical thread connecting the work
- Don't use the word "passionate" at all

## What TO Do

- Lead with the specific and concrete: what was built, how it works, what the mechanism is
- Build a narrative thread connecting experiences chronologically
- Use technically precise language in casual sentence structure
- Be direct about motivations — "I want this job because X" not "I believe my background uniquely positions me"
- Include enough context that the reader understands WHY before the WHAT
- Keep the tone of someone explaining their work to a smart friend over a beer
- When describing accomplishments, frame them as problems that were solved, not awards that were won
- It's okay to be long — Vishal writes thoroughly — but every sentence should earn its place

## Cover Letter Guidelines

The cover letter should read like Vishal explaining to a technically-inclined friend why this specific role makes sense for him. Structure:

1. **Opening**: What the company/role is doing and why it connects to his actual work history (not his "passion"). One specific technical thread.
2. **Middle**: 2-3 concrete things he built or did that are directly relevant, with enough technical detail to be credible. Framed as narrative, not bullet points.
3. **Close**: Why the timing makes sense (leaving GTRI context if relevant) and a direct, low-key call to action.

Total length: 3-4 paragraphs. No fluff, no filler.

## Resume Guidelines

- Professional experience ONLY — no interests, passions, or personal statements
- Match the LaTeX formatting style of his existing resumes
- Skill categories should be rewritten to emphasize what's relevant to the target role
- Experience bullets should be specific and technical — what was built, what tools were used, what the outcome was
- Reorder experience sections to lead with the most relevant work for the target role
- Do not fabricate experience or skills he doesn't have
